SHOVE
Part I — Origins

BEFORE
THE NAME

Maryland. Two high school friends. A shared record collection that begins and ends with the Jam, Elvis Costello, and the Velvet Underground. A band called Shove that no one remembers but both of them do.

Early 1990s

Two Maryland kids, one record collection

Andrew Pontano and Alec Boyajy are high school friends in Maryland. Bound by the Jam, Costello, the Velvet Underground. The songwriting partnership that defines everything starts here — in basements, in margins, on bus rides.

OriginsMaryland
Mid 1990s

Shove becomes Anne Summers

The band migrates to Arlington, VA and takes a new name designed to mislead. "Sounds like a blonde folksinger," the singer noted. All-male rock trio. Maximum misdirection. Maximum fun.

Arlington VAName Origin
Mid 1990s

The DC circuit — Black Cat, IOTA

Anne Summers find their crowd on the Northern Virginia and DC indie circuit. The Black Cat. IOTA. Their melodic, literate songs cut against the grunge hangover. Too clever for the slacker set, too pop for the post-hardcore crowd. Exactly right for everyone else.

Black CatIOTADC Scene
Mid 1990s

Paul Swegle — first drummer

Original drummer Paul Swegle completes the trio. Three-piece locked in: dual vocals, sharp guitar, compact songs that hit hard in under three minutes and demand another listen immediately after.

THE
DANDY

Bias Recording, Springfield VA. Latona Studios, Washington DC. September 23, 1997. Eleven tracks. Thirty-three minutes. The Washington Post reviewed it in January 1998.

Sept 23, 1997

The Dandy — PC Music

Eleven pop-punk songs. Velvet Underground modal guitar. Byrds riffs on "United Artists." Costello all over the vocals. The Stooges in the song titles. The whole thing sounds like a xerox of something great — deliberately rough, surprisingly moving.

Jan 1998

Washington Post reviews the debut

The Post notes the VU modal strum, Costello vocal fingerprints, Stooges title borrowings. "Pop-punk tracks on the Washington trio's debut." Legitimate local buzz. A band that deserved more attention starts getting some.

VERY
CLASSY

Beatville Records. Don Fleming producing. Water Music, Hoboken. Noon Tonight, Sterling VA. Brad Samuels on drums. A cellist named Donnelly Gillen. March 7, 2000.

1999–2000

Don Fleming produces

Don Fleming — Velvet Monkeys, Teenage Fanclub, Screaming Trees. He brings DC cred and real studio depth. Warmer recording, cello arrangements, bigger production. The sardonic core stays exactly where it was.

Very Classy — Beatville Records

Washington Post: "A contemporary Washington trio with just about everything it needs to be a '70s British new wave band — brisk delivery, catchy tunes, boatloads of self-deprecating rue." Opens with King of Disaster. Ends too soon.

Black Cat release show

Main stage. Brad Samuels was "breathing fire" backstage according to band lore. The drummer reportedly set himself on fire at the show's climax. Standard Arlington send-off. Jake Tapper was reportedly a regular at their shows.

2000

The amicable split

Anne Summers dissolve. Amicably. Concert Archives records they "split to pursue the 9-to-5 careers their lyrics lambasted." The most on-brand ending in Arlington music history. No drama. No reunion announced. Just gone.

THEY WERE

Guitar · Vocals

Co-founder. Maryland. Guitar riffs from VU drone to Byrds jangle. Primary songwriter alongside Boyajy. The band's guitar voice from first rehearsal to last show.

Co-founder. The dual-vocal approach was the whole secret — Costello and McCartney in one room, both writing, both singing, both ending up at a desk job anyway.

Context

THE DC
SCENE

Anne Summers arrived mid-90s into a Northern Virginia/DC indie ecosystem that included Unrest (Arlington, TeenBeat), Tsunami (also Arlington), and Wingtip Sloat (Falls Church) — and the shadow of the post-hardcore legacy that still defined what "DC music" meant.

They were none of those things. They were a tight power-pop trio with hooks, harmonies, and a very specific self-aware malaise — the mid-90s DC suburbs rendered as 33 minutes of punchy, literate, melodic noise.

The Black Cat was their home. The Post reviewed them twice. Jake Tapper, reportedly, was a regular. They were the best band in every room. You had to be there.
